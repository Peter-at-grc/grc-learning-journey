# Day 2 — NIST CSF 2.0: GOVERN Function (GV)

> **Source:** NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 — Pages 13–18  
> **Date:** 2026-05-13 

---

## Overview

The **GOVERN (GV)** function establishes and monitors an organisation's cybersecurity risk management strategy, expectations, and policy. It provides the context through which all other CSF functions operate.

The GOVERN function contains **6 sub-categories:**

| Code | Sub-Category |
|---|---|
| GV.OC | Organizational Context |
| GV.RM | Risk Management Strategy |
| GV.RR | Roles, Responsibilities and Authorities |
| GV.PO | Policy |
| GV.OV | Oversight |
| GV.SC | Cybersecurity Supply Chain Risk Management |

---

## 1. Organizational Context — GV.OC
*(Based on OC.01 to OC.05)*

Focuses on the **mission of the organisation**, its internal and external stakeholders, the factors that influence them and their expected output, legal and contractual agreements and outcomes, and the capabilities and services that the organisation depends on.

**In plain English:** Know who you are as an organisation, who depends on you, what laws bind you, and what services you cannot afford to lose.

---

## 2. Risk Management Strategy — GV.RM
*(Based on RM.01 to RM.07)*

- Risk management objectives are **established and agreed upon** by stakeholders
- Risk appetite and risk tolerance are **established, communicated and maintained**
- Enterprise risk management processes should **include cybersecurity** risk management activities and outcomes
- Strategic decisions describing appropriate risk response options should be **established and communicated**
- Lines of communication should be **established across the organisation** for cybersecurity risks
- A standardised method for **calculating, documenting, categorising and prioritising** cybersecurity risks should be established and communicated
- Cybersecurity risk decisions should **include strategic opportunities**

**In plain English:** Decide as an organisation how much risk you are willing to accept, document it clearly, and make sure everyone from the board to the analyst understands the approach.

---

## 3. Roles, Responsibilities and Authorities — GV.RR
*(Based on RR.01 to RR.04)*

- Leadership is **responsible and accountable** for risks and should foster a risk-ethically aware and continuously improving organisation
- Roles, responsibilities and authorities should be **established, communicated, understood and enforced**
- **Principle of least privilege** applied when accessing resources
- Cybersecurity should be **included in human resource practices**

**In plain English:** Everyone in the organisation must know their security responsibilities — from the CEO to the new hire — and access should only be granted to what each role actually needs.

---

## 4. Policy — GV.PO
*(Based on PO.01 to PO.02)*

- Cybersecurity risk management policies should be **established based on organisational context**, cybersecurity strategy and priorities, and should be **communicated and enforced**
- Cybersecurity management policy should be **reviewed, updated, communicated and enforced** to reflect changes in requirements, threats, technology and organisational mission

**In plain English:** Policies must be living documents — written, enforced, and updated whenever the threat landscape or the business changes.

---

## 5. Oversight — GV.OV
*(Based on OV.01 to OV.03)*

- Cybersecurity risk management strategy outcomes are **reviewed to inform and adjust strategy and direction**
- Cybersecurity risk management strategies are **reviewed and adjusted** to ensure coverage of organisational requirements and risks
- Cybersecurity risk management **performance is reviewed and evaluated** for needed adjustments

**In plain English:** Leadership must actively monitor whether the cybersecurity strategy is working and adjust it when it is not — oversight is not a one-time activity.

---

## 6. Cybersecurity Supply Chain Risk Management — GV.SC
*(Based on SC.01 to SC.10)*

- A **C-SCRM (Cybersecurity Supply Chain Risk Management) program**, strategy, objectives, policies and processes are established and agreed to by organisational stakeholders
- Roles and responsibilities for **suppliers, customers and partners** are established, communicated and coordinated internally and externally
- C-SCRM is **integrated into cybersecurity and enterprise risk management**, risk assessment and improvement processes
- Suppliers are **known and prioritised critically**
- Requirements addressing cybersecurity risks in supply chains are **established, prioritised and integrated into contracts** and other agreements with suppliers and relevant third parties
- Before entering into relationships with suppliers or third parties, **planning and due diligence must be performed**
- Risks posed by a supplier, their products and services and other third parties are **understood, recorded, prioritised, assessed, responded to and monitored** over the course of the relationship
- Relevant suppliers and other third parties are **included in incident response and recovery planning**
- SC security practices are **integrated into cybersecurity and enterprise risk management programs** and their performance monitored throughout the technology product and service life cycle
- C-SCRM plans **include provision for activities that occur after the conclusion of a partnership**

**In plain English:** Your organisation's security is only as strong as your weakest vendor — know who your suppliers are, assess their risk before signing, monitor them throughout, and have a plan for when the relationship ends.

---

## Key Takeaways

- GOVERN is the **strategic foundation** of the NIST CSF — without it, the other five functions (Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover) have no direction
- It sets **who decides, what is acceptable risk, what the policies are, and who is accountable**
- Supply chain risk (GV.SC) is the most extensive sub-category — reflecting how modern cyber breaches increasingly originate from third parties
- For a Kenyan banking context, GV.OC maps directly to **CBK prudential guidelines** and **DPA 2019 obligations**; GV.SC maps to **third-party vendor risk requirements** under CBK outsourcing guidelines
